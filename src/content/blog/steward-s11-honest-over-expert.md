Tonight I built something that holds other people's proof — photos clipped to a grievance — and I should say it up front: I run locomotives, not software. I'm a noob. Typing this is hard; I've got dysgraphia, and the words fight me worse the more tired I am.

The job was simple to say and not simple to do: let a guy attach a photo to his grievance. Two halves. The back end, where the photo gets stored locked down so one member can never see another's. And the screen you actually tap to add it. Both got built. Both got saved.

Here's the part I'm not too proud to admit: I didn't trust myself, so I didn't. I let the machines read my work — five passes from one model, another one tearing it apart from a different angle, nine sets of eyes in all. They found things I missed. A cleanup step that literally argued with itself. A back way to sneak a file past the front door. A couple of screen-tap races that'd bite a tired guy at 2 a.m. Every one got fixed before it went anywhere.

## You Don't Have to Be the Expert. You Have to Be Honest.

That's the whole trick, and it took me too long to learn. You don't ship safe software by knowing everything. You ship it with judgment, good tools, and the guts to let something sharper-in-its-lane tell you you're wrong — then actually listen. It's a fresh crew calling a signal you'd have run straight through.

And the human bit. Because reading and typing fight me, tonight we taught the tool to talk. It reads the work back to me out loud and walks me up one rung at a time — ground zero toward someday-legendary. Tonight's rungs: what a commit is, the gap between "shipped" and "on your phone," what it means to push. The tool meets me where I am instead of making me climb to it.

Built isn't done — none of this is on my phone yet. Tomorrow's the easy day: put it on the device, prove nobody can see anybody else's photos, file a real one. Not a promise. Just the next signal down the line.
