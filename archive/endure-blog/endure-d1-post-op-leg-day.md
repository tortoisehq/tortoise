I'm 35. I had a microdiscectomy. My left leg has claudication, which means the nerve starts complaining after about ten minutes of walking and makes grocery shopping feel like a cardio event.

I'm also 24 months into a training push. Those two facts are not friends, and I'm not going to force them to be. I'm going to make them work in the same room, most days, with a fair amount of honest paperwork.

This blog is the paperwork.

```
Bodyweight:    206.6 lbs
Sleep:         9.5h (after 4h two nights ago)
Energy:        8/10
Pain (L leg):  3/10, stable the whole session
Volume:        37 sets · 697 reps · ~40,800 lbs
Top sets:      Leg extension 250×9 · Leg curl 145×5
Duration:      64 min
```

Hamstring-to-quad ratio is up to 58% from 50% last week. I brought that up on purpose. I'm pretending I didn't Google "post-microdiscectomy hamstring weakness" three times in a row last Sunday.

## What today is actually about

**Sleep recovery.** I got four hours two nights ago. I got nine and a half last night. Today's workout happened because of the second number, not in spite of the first. You can't train on a short night. You can train on the rebound. These feel like the same thing until they are extremely not.

**Pain management.** Three out of ten, stable, no escalation. That's the credible range. Not pain-free — I have given up on pain-free as a planning assumption — but not climbing either. The goal is knowing where the ceiling is and stopping before the nerve writes me a strongly worded letter.

**Volume pacing.** I could probably do three or four times this work. I don't. The math on post-op nerve issues is not generous — they punish overtraining harder than they reward it, and the reward tops out quickly anyway. The win is the set I did. The bigger win is the set I didn't. A year ago I did not understand this. Now I am apparently writing blog posts about it.

## The shift-work part

Most fitness content assumes you wake up at 6 a.m. feeling like a person, drink a green smoothie, and have eight clean uninterrupted hours of rest behind you. A lot of us don't. Shift rotations, night rail work, four hours of sleep on a Tuesday that bleeds into a Wednesday I'd rather not describe — that's the actual terrain.

The rule I've landed on: you don't have to be great. You just have to do what the session allows. Some weeks that's 40,000 pounds across 37 sets. Some weeks that's a bike and two stretches and going home. Both count. The streak is showing up, not overperforming.

Day 1 logged. See you tomorrow, or the day after, or whenever the next shift lets me.
